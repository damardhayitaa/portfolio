01 — Context

A product that took months to launch — then went dark

Government integrations are never simple. This one involved a long process: proof of concept, regulatory alignment, technical integration, UAT, and finally — go-live. By the time the product launched, the team had been deep in execution mode for months.

But the moment it went live, a different problem emerged: nobody had a reliable way to monitor whether it was actually working. There was no dashboard. No shared view. No agreed-upon metrics to watch. The product existed, but its health was invisible.

The gap I identified

We had gone through an extensive process to get the product live — but had no plan for what happens after. No one was watching. And if something broke, we wouldn't know until a user or a partner flagged it.

02 — Problem Framing

Defining what "visibility" actually means

Before going to the data team with a request, I needed to define exactly what needed to be monitored — and why. A vague brief produces a vague dashboard. So I structured the monitoring requirements into five distinct areas, each tied to a specific question the team needed to answer.

My brief to the data team

"Our main objective is to track adoption and understand how users move through the journey — and identify where adoption or failures might occur."

I then broke this down into five concrete areas:

01

Adoption Funnel

  • Users selecting the product as certificate provider
  • Certificate request attempts (account linking)
  • Certificates successfully issued
  • Users who complete signing
02

Growth Metrics

  • Cumulative certificates linked to Coretax
  • Daily new certificate issuance
  • Daily signing volume
03

Transaction Performance

  • Successful vs. failed signing transactions
  • Signing success rate
  • Unique signing users per day/week
04

Conversion Rates

  • Selected → certificate issued
  • Certificate issued → first signing
  • Signing attempt → successful signing
05

Reliability Monitoring

  • Signing failures by reason (OTP failure, certificate error, etc.)
  • Overall error rate trend

Nobody had asked these questions yet — because everyone assumed someone else was watching. I escalated this gap to leadership, presented the structured brief, and proposed the monitoring dashboard as the immediate next step.

"The integration was a success on paper. But 'live' doesn't mean 'healthy' — and nobody had a way to tell the difference."
03 — My Approach

Directing without building it myself

This was a case where the right move wasn't to build the dashboard myself — it was to define exactly what needed to be built, and pair closely with the data team to make it happen.

1

Wrote a structured brief for the data team

Rather than saying "we need a dashboard," I defined five specific monitoring areas: adoption funnel, growth metrics, transaction performance, conversion rates, and reliability monitoring — each with concrete metrics and the question it was meant to answer.

2

Identified the right stakeholder audience

Different teams needed different views. Business ops needed high-level volume and uptime. Engineering needed error details. Leadership needed trend summaries. I defined which metrics belonged in which view before a single chart was built.

3

Paired with the data analyst to align on approach

Worked directly with the data analyst to walk through the requirements, clarify data sources, and make decisions together on metric definitions. This wasn't a one-way hand-off — I was involved enough to catch misalignments early.

4

Reviewed iterations and gave structured feedback

The data team built in Holistic. I reviewed each version against the original requirements — flagging missing metrics, unclear labels, and charts that didn't answer the right question. Two rounds of revision before sign-off.

5

Socialized the dashboard across teams

Once live, I walked each stakeholder group through the dashboard — explaining what each metric meant, what to watch for, and who to escalate to if something looked off. Made sure it was actually used, not just built.

Key decision

I chose to use Holistic (the company's existing BI tool) rather than proposing a new tool — reducing friction for adoption. The best dashboard is the one people actually open.

04 — Outcomes

From invisible to observable

Full visibility post-launch

For the first time, the team had a shared, reliable view of the integration's health — updated in real time.

Cross-team alignment

Business, product, and ops teams were now looking at the same numbers — reducing the "whose data is right?" conversation in every sync.

Proactive issue detection

Errors and anomalies became visible before they escalated into user complaints or partner inquiries — giving the team time to respond.

Operational confidence

Leadership and business ops could answer questions about product performance without pulling in the PM or engineering — self-serve monitoring became the norm.

05 — What I Learned

Lessons I'm taking forward

01

Launch is not the finish line — observability is

A product that nobody can monitor is functionally incomplete. I now treat post-launch monitoring as a launch requirement, not an afterthought. It goes in the PRD from day one.

02

Directing well is a skill, not a shortcut

Working through the data team meant I had to be precise about requirements. Vague briefs produce vague dashboards. I learned to define the "so what" for every metric before asking anyone to build it.

03

Adoption requires socialization, not just delivery

Building the dashboard wasn't enough — I had to make sure people knew it existed, knew how to read it, and knew what to do with it. A dashboard nobody opens doesn't solve anything.
